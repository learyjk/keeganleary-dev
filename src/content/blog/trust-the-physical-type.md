People assume the monospace-and-dot-matrix look on this site is an aesthetic choice first and a functional one second. It's the other way around. A fixed-width grid tells your eye exactly where a column starts and ends before you've read a single word. Dotted rules read as "cut here" or "tear here" instantly, because every human who has ever used a paper form already knows that grammar. I didn't invent the shorthand, I just borrowed it.

## Constraints as a feature, not a limitation

Real printers, typewriters, and terminals all shipped with brutal constraints — fixed character widths, limited color, mechanical timing. Designers who worked within those constraints produced some of the clearest information design ever made, because there was no room for a stray gradient to paper over a bad hierarchy. If the layout didn't work in twelve characters, it didn't work.

I try to hold my own work to the same standard digitally, even though I have effectively unlimited pixels available. Can this hierarchy survive being reduced to a fixed-width grid with two colors? If yes, it's probably sound. If it only works because of a drop shadow and a hover animation doing the heavy lifting, it's not done yet.

## Where I break the rule

The one place I let modern easing back in is motion between states — like this very page transition. The static grid stays mechanical and honest. The movement between two mechanical states is where I allow myself an interpolation the original hardware never could have rendered. Old bones, new joints.
