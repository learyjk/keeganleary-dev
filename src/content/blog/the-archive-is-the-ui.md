Every portfolio I have ever built starts the same way: a big friendly headline, a soft-focus photo, three case studies with the messy parts sanded off. It works. It also lies a little. The interesting decisions happen in the drafts, the postmortems, the version that shipped at 2 a.m. because the client moved the deadline again.

So this site does the unglamorous thing instead. The archive is not a footnote — it is the layout. The feed holes on the left aren't decoration, they're a reminder that this is a printout, not a brochure. Nothing here is trying to look more finished than it is.

## Why a printer, not a gallery

Galleries curate. Printers record. A gallery wants you to see the best angle of the sculpture. A printer just keeps going until it runs out of paper, timestamps included. I wanted the site to behave like the second thing — a running log that happens to look good, rather than a highlight reel pretending to be a log.

That's also why clicking into a post doesn't take you to a brand new page that forgets where you came from. The title and the metadata — date, topic, read time, status — travel with you. They peel off the row you clicked and resettle in the left column, the same slot where the bio used to sit. The bio hasn't gone anywhere permanent; it just stepped out so the post could take its shift.

## What "New" actually means

The little dot next to "New" isn't a marketing trick. It means the postmortem hasn't been cross-referenced against the next three projects yet — the lessons are still hot. Everything marked "Filed" has already been checked against at least one later project to see if the lesson actually held up. If it didn't, I say so. That's the whole point of keeping the archive instead of hiding it.
